rescyou

protecting lifes with UX

The smart warning triangle from rescyou is designed to support drivers in case of a breakdown or accident with excellent UX so to relieve them as much as possible. It provides pleasant support in an unpleasant situation.


Let’s be honest — the road traffic regulations in Germany and abroad can be complicated. In a stressful situation, hardly anyone knows exactly how far the warning triangle must be placed from the car. Rescyou eliminates this uncertainty completely. The further it is moved away from the car, the more an LED strip fills up until the correct distance is reached and a second indicator signals to set the triangle on the ground. And it simply works automatically, like magic. There is a B2C version that can be installed in any car, whereas the B2B version is integrated in the trunk trim panel by the car manufacturer.


The project was developed as part of a university project together with Fabian Lempke. The renderings were created in Cinema 4D, the microsite was built with webflow. For more details—feel free to check it out!

Danger Zone

in-car UX with Porsche

In this one week design sprint my team and I put Porsches new in-house built prototyping and storytelling tool to the test. Therefore we deleloped a new potential feature for their new Porsche Mission E Cross Turismo. Protecting the driver in a situation of first responder at a crash sight. It was important to consider not only the interior screens, but the whole car with all its sensors, phyisical feedbacks, and indicators. In an extreme situation like the one shown, it was very important to guide the drivers view very percisely and just giving him the information needed.


Insights and research

Our initial approach was to tackle the topic »first aid«. Since even in our team of young drivers, no one was confident about the procedures. And our research with people in the average Porsche driver age only confirmed our assumptions. But when we turned to the department of the firefighters Potsdam, we learned, that inexpert first aid is rarely a problem nowadays – but self protection at the crash scene is! Quote: »If you’re dead yourself, you can't help anyone else«!


Idea

Therefore we changed our focus from assisting first responders to help others to assist the first responders protecting themselves. In our further research we shadowed people while they handled an improvised crash scene situation. The learnings of this shadowing combined with the insights provided by the fire fighters was the foundation on witch we built »Danger Zone« – our Storytelling and Prototyping approach to keep first responders safe.


The course »Future In-Car UX Design« was a collaboration between the University of Applied Sciences Potsdam and the Porsche UX department.

The Porsche Mission E Cross Turismo

Michael, 47, is on his way home …

When suddenly …

Situation

Michael drives his Porsche around a corner, suddenly passes an accident and instictively hits the breakes.

Michael's mind

»NO! Not good! not good!«

The car

The cameras detected the crash as well – causing the car to switch in »first responder mode«. To put some pressure off of Michael right away, the ambulance is requested autonomously. The hazard lights are activated as well.

As the car stoppped …

Situation

Kind of shocked, Michael stops his Porsche right behind the corner and tries to unbuckle his seatbelt to jump out of his car.


Michael's mind

»Shit, what happened here? Is someone inside? Maybe dead? I got to go there and help them!«


The car

The car detects the car's life threatening position and gives instructions of where to park instead. The car's AI uses camera and location footage to map the scenery and to calculate the „Danger Zone“. As Michael ignores all instructions and tries to unbuckle his seatbelt, small engines in the buckle make it harder to open and give him direct haptical feedback to stay fastened, while voice instructions urge him to follow the navigation a second time – now more personally.

About thirty meter down the road …



Situation

As Michael has recovered from the first shock, he follows the navigation to the suggested parking position outside the marked Danger Zone.


Michael's mind

»Alright, Whats next? What have I learned back then? Ah… warning triangle!«


The car

Like most drivers his age, Michael forgets about the safety vest which is only compulsory since less than 10 years. He’s in a way better parking position, but he'd be still in danger once he’d stepped out of the car without the vest. That’s why the first responder mode reminds him.

Once the vest is applied …



Situation

Michael puts on the vest and waits for next instructions.


Michael's mind

»But now it’s definitely the warning triangle. How is it again? 30 m? No, 100 m! Maybe more as it’s outside a village… hmm….«


The car

Michael has arrived at the last step of the first responder mode — The warning triangle. The Cluster Screen shows him where to find it, while the map is giving him an idea of where to set it up.

Michael sets up the smart triangle …



Situation

He gets a kit containing of the warning triangle and the medical set out of the trunk and walks along the street to set up the triangle.


Michael's mind

»Okay, I’ll set it up somewhere in front of the corner… like shown on the screen. But then what? How am I supposed to help the injured? When will the ambulance arrive?«


The car

The moment Michael removes the kit from its position in the car, it automatically switches into active mode. The smart warning triangle illuminates to protect Michael on his way down the street and vibrates gently once he reaches the right position. The smart medical kit on the other hand is equipped with an emergency phone and automatically connects to the approaching ambulance. The kit can be separated. Therefore Michael can leave the warning triangle at the road while taking his call and the medical set to the crash scene.

allgood

a peer to peer sick pay cover

»allgood« is a p2p sick pay cover for freelancers. It is the result of crafting a finance related product, that on one hand builds trust while on the other hand contributing to a better financial education. It was expected to be based on research, interviews and user testing rather than hypes or assumptions. Some exploration in the field of the lately booming freelance economy led to some main insight:


About 16% of all European labor is already done freelance. But traditional safeguards for times of sickness or injuries have fall short for the self-employed.


Current studies conclude: Self-employed people take about three times fewer days off work for illness than traditional employees. Mostly due to the fear of financial losses. Through our research we found fairly good solutions already tackling the problem offline. Like the Dutch »broodfonds«, which connects freelancers in small, local communities to cover each other during periods of illness. So we went one step further and thought about a digital solution for the digital generation:


An app that builds and maintains a small, trusted community of freelancers who solidary cover for each other in case they get sick.

Swapp

change the game

Browse through video games in the neighbourhood, connect with their owners and simply swapp games - instead of buying new ones.


No other entertainment industry today is as powerful & influential as the computer and console industry. Due to the oversupply of games and possibilities in this area, the user is often faced with countless decisions. The price often plays a major role. Despite their good storylines, many games have a limited number of missions and not every game can be played online against other players for an unlimited period of time. In the long run the player needs new challenges, i.e. new games. Although there are already portals for the direct download of new games on the Playstation, Xbox and Co. platforms, the hardcover versions of these games are unlikely to decrease significantly in the coming years.

bloom

understanding team bottlenecks

»Supported by bloom« is a platform solution that foster team culture and continuous learning by connecting teams with the workshop mediators they need based on data. Weekly team feedback provided by blooms assessment tool enables the platform to identify weaknesses in team structures and, in the next step, to propose workshops that address these weaknesses. No subjectively chosen workshops by the team lead anymore. No hours of researching workshop formats. The data speaks for itself.


In the digital economy, platforms are transforming industries at high speed. By eliminating traditional gatekeepers, 2-sided platforms reduce search efforts, facilitate matching, and make it easier for two or more economic agents to exchange value between each other. Assuming varying structures, numerous forms of value creating and spanning practically every economy sector, platforms are highly complex systems. To gain a deeper understanding, using user-centered-design is one practical way to understand user and market needs. To gain platform expertise applying all learnings and designing one is a must.


Bloom was built within a course at FH Potsdam »Designing two-sided platforms from a designers perspective« and is a concept, in which my team and I were faking a landing page to evaluate the idea through google and LinkedIn ads by counting generated leads.

Data Resonance Imaging

exploring activity of shared directories visually

On a computer, shared folders have become the backbone for collaborative working. A regular file system, however, doesn’t live up to the demands of a productive collaborative space. It is no longer enough to know where a file is located. Users need more contextual information like who was working when on which file. Furthermore, jumping back in the project timeline and reading the communication in the team that is related to specific files is often essential for understanding and modifying the current state of the project.


This problem was the starting point for the Data Resonance Imaging project. The project investigates how a visual representation of file properties and histories as well as an associated group chats can support users in understanding and using the file structure.


Data Resonance Imaging creates a visual model of a »project flow«. New files are created, old ones deleted. As the activity shifts from file A to file B, collaboration patterns emerge. With DRI, it is possible to target specific temporal points in the file system history and examine the context of the selected time frame. Keywords and highlights from the group chat provide the users with contextual information. The visual representation of file properties like »activity« (the amount of file operations per time frame) support the understanding of the project flow.
